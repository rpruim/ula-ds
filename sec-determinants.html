    Section 4.5 Determinants

    As invertibility plays a central role in this chapter, we need a criterion that tells us when a matrix is invertible. We already know that a square matrix is invertible if and only if it is row equivalent to the identity matrix. In this section, we will develop a second, numerical criterion that tells us when a square matrix is invertible.
    To begin, let‚Äôs consider a \(2\by2\) matrix \(A\) whose columns are vectors \(\vvec_1\) and \(\vvec_2\text{.}\) We have frequently drawn the vectors and studied the linear combinations they form using a figure such as Figure¬†4.5.1.
    Notice how the linear combinations form a set of congruent parallelograms in the plane. In this section, we will use the area of these parallelograms to define a numerical quantity called the determinant that tells us whether the matrix \(A\) is invertible.
    To recall, the area of parallelogram is found by multiplying the length of one side by the perpendicular distance to its parallel side. Using the notation in the figure, the area of the parallelogram is \(bh\text{.}\)

    Subsection 4.5.1 Determinants of \(2\by2\) matrices

    Determinants are defined for any square matrix, but we will begin by defining the determinant of a \(2\by2\) matrix \(A = \left[\begin{array}{rr} \vvec_1 \amp \vvec_2 \end{array}\right]\text{.}\) First, however, we need to define the orientation of an ordered pair of vectors. As shown in Figure¬†4.5.2, an ordered pair of vectors \(\vvec_1\) and \(\vvec_2\) is called positively oriented if the angle, measured in the counterclockwise direction, from \(\vvec_1\) to \(\vvec_2\) is less than \(180^\circ\text{;}\) we say the pair is negatively oriented if it is more than \(180^\circ\text{.}\)

    Definition 4.5.3.

    Suppose a \(2\by2\) matrix \(A\) has columns \(\vvec_1\) and \(\vvec_2\text{.}\) If the pair of vectors is positively oriented, then the determinant of \(A\text{,}\) denoted \(\det(A)\text{,}\) is the area of the parallelogram formed by \(\vvec_1\) and \(\vvec_2\text{.}\) If the pair is negatively oriented, then \(\det(A)\) is minus the area of the parallelogram.

    Example 4.5.4.

    Consider the determinant of the identity matrix
    \begin{equation*} I = \left[\begin{array}{rr} 1\amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1 \\ \end{array}\right] = \left[\begin{array}{rr} \evec_1 \amp \evec_2 \\ \end{array}\right]\text{.} \end{equation*}
    As seen on the left of Figure¬†4.5.5, the vectors \(\vvec_1 = \evec_1\) and \(\vvec_2=\evec_2\) form a positively oriented pair. Since the parallelogram they form is a \(1\by1\) square, we have \(\det(I) = 1.\)
    Now consider the matrix
    \begin{equation*} A = \left[\begin{array}{rr} -2\amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1 \\ \end{array}\right] = \left[\begin{array}{rr} \vvec_1 \amp \vvec_2 \\ \end{array}\right]\text{.} \end{equation*}
    As seen on the right of Figure¬†4.5.5, the vectors \(\vvec_1\) and \(\vvec_2\) form a negatively oriented pair. The parallelogram they define is a \(2\by1\) rectangle so we have \(\det(A) = -2\text{.}\)

    1. The determinant is \(-1\) because the vectors are negatively oriented and the rectangle has sides of length \(\frac12\) and \(2\text{.}\) The determinant of a diagonal matrix seems to be the product of the diagonal entries.
    2. The matrix transformation is a reflection over the line \(y=x\) and we see that the determinant is \(-1\text{.}\)
    3. The determinant will continue to be \(2\) for any value of \(k\text{.}\) This illustrates the fact that the determinant of an upper triangular matrix equals the product of its diagonal entries.
    4. The same reasoning tells us that this determinant is \(2\) and, in fact, the determinant of a lower triangular matrix equals the product of its diagonal entries.
    5. The determinant of this matrix is \(0\) because the parallelogram formed by the vector has no area. This suggests that the determinant of a matrix whose columns are linearly dependent is \(0\text{.}\)
    6. We find that \(\det(A) = -4\text{,}\) \(\det(B) = 2\text{,}\) and \(\det(AB) = -8\text{.}\) This suggests that \(\det(AB) = \det(A) \det(B)\text{.}\)
    Activity¬†4.5.2 illustrates some important properties of determinants that are true more generally, including for larger matrices.
    • If \(A\) is a diagonal matrix, then \(\det(A)\) equals the product of the entries on the diagonal. A \(2 \by 2\) diagonal matrix represents a transformation that scales horizontally and vertically by the scaling factors on the diagonal. The area of the transformed unit square is the product of the diagonal entries. If exactly one of the two is negative, the orientation is reversed and the determinant will be negative. We saw an example of this in Figure¬†4.5.5.
    • If \(A\) is a triangular matrix, then \(\det(A)\) equals the product of the entries on the diagonal. A \(2 \by 2\) triangular matrix represents a shear transformation. The diagonal entries represent the horizontal and vertical scaling. The non-zero off-diagonal entry represents the amount of shear. This distorts a rectangle into a parallelogram, but leaves the area unchanged. For example,
      \begin{equation*} \det\left[\begin{array}{rr} 2 \amp 2 \\ 0 \amp 3 \\ \end{array}\right] = 2\cdot 3 = 6\text{,} \end{equation*}
      since the two parallelograms in Figure¬†4.5.7 have equal area.
    • We also saw that
      \begin{equation*} \det \left[\begin{array}{rr} 0 \amp 1 \\ 1 \amp 0 \\ \end{array}\right] = -1 \end{equation*}
      because the columns form a negatively oriented pair. You may remember from Section¬†4.2 that a matrix such as this is obtained by interchanging two rows of the identity matrix.
    • The determinant satisfies a multiplicative property, which says that
      \begin{equation*} \det(AB) = \det(A)\det(B). \end{equation*}
      Rather than simply thinking of the determinant as the area of a parallelogram, we may also think of it as a factor by which areas are scaled under the matrix transformation defined by the matrix. Applying the matrix transformation defined by \(B\) will scale area by \(\det(B)\text{.}\) If we then compose \(B\) with the matrix transformation defined by \(A\text{,}\) area will scale a second time by the factor \(\det(A)\text{.}\) The net effect is that the matrix transformation defined by \(AB\) scales area by \(\det(A)\det(B)\) so that \(\det(AB)=\det(A)\det(B)\text{.}\)
    As we will see, these properties hold for larger matrices as well.

    Subsection 4.5.2 Determinants of larger matrices

    We can define determinants for \(n\by n\) matrices by measuring the "volume" of a "box" (technically a parallelepiped) defined by the columns of the matrix, even if this box resides in \(\real^n\) for some very large \(n\text{.}\)
    For example, the columns of a \(3\by3\) matrix \(A\) will form a parallelpiped, like the one shown here, and there is a means by which we can classify sets of such vectors as either positively or negatively oriented. Therefore, we can define the determinant \(\det(A)\) in terms of the volume of the parallelpiped, but we will not worry about the details here.
    Soon we will learn general methods for computing the determinant of a matrix, but some determinants are easy to calculate directly from the definition.

    Proposition 4.5.8.

    1. \(\det(I) = 1\) for any identity matrix \(I\text{.}\)
    2. If \(A\) is a square matrix with a row of 0s, then \(\det(A) = 0\text{.}\)

    Proof.

    1. The identity transformation doesn‚Äôt change anything, so the scaling factor is 1.
    2. If row \(r\) of \(A\) consists entirely of 0s, then the \(r\)th component of \(A \xvec\) will be 0. This means that the transformation associated with \(A\) collapses into a smaller space, and so the volume of any transformed shape will be 0.
    Several properties established for \(2\by2\) matrices hold for larger matrices as well. In particular the following proposition holds by the same argument given for \(2 \by 2\) matrices.

    Proposition 4.5.9.

    For any \(n \by n\) matrices \(A\) and \(B\text{,}\)
    \begin{equation*} \det(AB) = \det(A)\det(B)\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Proposition¬†4.5.9 allows us to establish a relationship between the determinant of an invertible matrix and the determinant of its inverse. For an invertible matrix \(A\) we know that \(\det(A) \det(A^{-1}) = \det(A A^{-1}) = \det(I) = 1\text{.}\) This leads to the following proposition.

    Proposition 4.5.10.

    If \(A\) is an invertible matrix, then \(\det(A^{-1}) = 1/\det(A)\text{.}\)
    Perhaps the most important property of determinants also appeared in Activity¬†4.5.2. We saw that when the columns of the matrix \(A\) are linearly dependent, the parallelogram formed by those vectors folds down onto a line. For instance, if \(A=\begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 2 \\ -1 \amp -2 \\ \end{bmatrix}\text{,}\) then the resulting parallelogram, as shown in Figure¬†4.5.11, has zero area, which means that \(\det(A)=0\text{.}\)
    The condition that the columns of \(A\) are linearly dependent is equivalent same to the condition that \(A\) is not invertible. This leads us to believe that \(A\) is not invertible if and only if its determinant is zero. The following proposition expresses this thought.

    Proposition 4.5.12.

    The matrix \(A\) is invertible if and only if \(\det(A) \neq 0\text{.}\)
    To understand this proposition more fully, let‚Äôs remember that the matrix \(A\) is invertible if and only if it is row equivalent to the identity matrix \(I\text{.}\) We will therefore consider how the determinant changes when we perform row operations on a matrix. Along the way, we will discover an effective means to compute the determinant.

    Subsubsection 4.5.2.1 Determinants of elementary matrices

    In Section¬†4.2, we saw how to describe the three row operations, scaling, interchange, and replacement, using matrix multiplication. Now we would like to compute the determinants of each of these matrices.
    • Scaling.
      Scalings are performed by multiplying a matrix \(A\) by a diagonal matrix, such as
      \begin{equation*} S = \left[\begin{array}{rrr} 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 3 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \\ \end{array}\right], \end{equation*}
      which has the effect of multiplying the second row of \(A\) by \(3\) to obtain \(A'\text{.}\) The associated transformation scales by a factor of three in one dimension, so it scales the volumne by 3 as well. Thus \(\det(S) = 3\text{.}\) More generally, if we scale any row of \(A\) \(k\text{,}\) we have
      \begin{equation*} \det(S) = k\text{.} \end{equation*}
      Note that when \(k\) is negative, the orientation is reversed by scaling.
    • Interchange.
      Interchanges are performed by matrices \(P\) which result from interchanging two rows of the identity matrix. For example,
      \begin{equation*} P = \left[\begin{array}{rrr} 0 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 1 \\ \end{array}\right], \end{equation*}
      interchanges the first and second rows of \(I_3\text{.}\) Note that for any such matrix \(P\text{,}\) \(P P = I\text{,}\) so \(\det(P) \det(P) = 1\text{.}\) This means that \(\det(P)\) must either be \(1\) or \(-1\text{.}\) As a transformation, an interchange "swaps" two axes. This reverses the orientation, so
      \begin{equation*} \det(P) = -1 \end{equation*}
      for any interchange matrix \(P\text{.}\)
    • Replacement.
      Row replacement operations are performed by matrices such as
      \begin{equation*} R = \left[\begin{array}{rrr} 1 \amp 0 \amp 0 \\ 0 \amp 1 \amp 0 \\ -2 \amp 0 \amp 1 \\ \end{array}\right], \end{equation*}
      which multiplies the first row by \(-2\) and adds the result to the third row. Each such matrix has exactly one off-diagonal entry that is non-zero. This represents a shear along one axis of \(\real^n\text{,}\) so
      \begin{equation*} \det(R) = 1 \end{equation*}
      for any replacement matrix \(R\text{.}\)
    Proposition 4.5.13. The determinants of elementary matrices.
    • If \(S\) is a scaling matrix with scaling factor \(k\text{,}\) then \(\det(S) = k\text{.}\)
    • If \(P\) is an interchange matrix, then \(\det(P) = -1\text{.}\)
    • If \(R\) is a replacement matrix, then \(\det(R) = 1\text{.}\)

    Subsubsection 4.5.2.2 Using RREF to compute determinants

    Proposition¬†4.5.13 allows us to compute the determinant of any matrix. If \(A\) is invertible, then \(A\sim I\text{.}\) But this means that
    \begin{equation*} E_k E_{k-1} \cdots E_2 E_1 A = I \end{equation*}
    for some elementary matrices \(E_1, E_2, \dots, E_k\text{.}\) By Proposition¬†4.5.9 this implies
    \begin{equation*} \det(E_k) \det(E_{k-1}) \cdots \det(E_2) \det(E_1) \det(A) = \det(I) = 1\text{,} \end{equation*}
    so
    \begin{equation*} \det(A) = \frac{1}{\det(E_k) \det(E_{k-1}) \cdots \det(E_2) \det(E_1)}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Recall that \(\det(R) = 1\) for an replacement matrices, so most of these terms are 1. Also \(\det(P) = -1\text{,}\) so we only need to know if we have an even or odd number of interchanges to know if the determinant is positive or negative. The abosulte value of the determinant of \(A\) is the reciprocal of the product of the scaling factors used in any scaling operations involved in computing the RREF.
    If \(A\) is not invertible, then the RREF of \(A\) will have a row of zeros, so by Proposition¬†4.5.8,
    \begin{equation*} \det(E_k) \det(E_{k-1}) \cdots \det(E_2) \det(E_1) \det(A) = 0\text{,} \end{equation*}
    from which we see that \(\det(A) = 0\text{.}\)
    So with just a little bookkeeping, we can compute the determinant of a matrix while we are calculting its reduced row echelon form.
    Proposition 4.5.14. The effect of row operations on the determinant.
    • If \(A'\) is obtained from \(A\) by scaling a row by \(k\text{,}\) then \(\det(A') = k\det(A)\text{.}\)
    • If \(A'\) is obtained from \(A\) by interchanging two rows, then \(\det(A') = -\det(A)\text{.}\)
    • If \(A'\) is obtained from \(A\) by performing a replacement operation, then \(\det(A') = \det(A)\text{.}\)
    Elemenatary matrices also provide us a tool for demonstrating Proposition¬†4.5.15 and Proposition¬†4.5.12.
    Proposition 4.5.15.
    The determinant of a square triangular matrix equals the product of its diagonal entries.
    Proof.
    If \(A\) is an \(n \by n\) triangular matrix, then we can reduce \(A\) to an \(n \by n\) diagonal matrix \(D\) using only replacement operations. Thus \(\det(A) = \det(D)\text{.}\) Also, the diagonal entries of \(D\) are the same as the diagonal entries of \(A\text{.}\)
    A square diagonal matrix \(D\) can be reduced to \(I\) by a sequence of scaling operations. If the diagonal entires of \(D\) are \(d_1, d_2, \dots d_n\text{,}\) then we must scale by \(1/d_1, 1/d_2, \dots 1/d_n\text{,}\) so
    \begin{equation*} \frac{1}{d_1} \cdot \frac{1}{d_2} \dots \frac{1}{d_n} \det(D) = \det(I) = 1 \end{equation*}
    and
    \begin{equation*} \det(D) = d_1 d_2 \cdots d_n\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Proof of Proposition¬†4.5.12.
    If \(A\) is invertible, then \(A \sim I\text{.}\) So \(\det(A)\) is a nonzero multiple of \(\det(I) = 1\text{.}\) Thus \(\det(A) \neq 0\text{.}\)
    If \(A\) is not invertible, then \(A \sim B\) for some matrix \(B\) that contains a row of 0s. This means \(\det(A)\) is a nonzero multiple of \(\det(B) = 0\text{.}\) Thus \(\det(A) = 0\text{.}\)

    Subsubsection 4.5.2.3 Cofactor expansions

    We now have a technique for computing the determinant of a matrix using row operations. There is another way to compute determinants, using what are called cofactor expansions, that will be important for us in the next chapter. We will describe this method here.
    To begin, let‚Äôs show how to compute the determinant of a \(2\by2\) matrix.
    Determinant of a \(2 \by 2 \) matrix.
    Let \(A = \left[\begin{array}{rr} a \amp b \\ c \amp d \\ \end{array}\right] \text{.}\) We can create an equivalent triangular matrix using \(-\frac{c}{a} R_1 + R_2 \to R_2\text{.}\) This gives us
    \begin{equation*} \det A = \det \left[\begin{array}{rr} a \amp b \\ c \amp d \\ \end{array}\right] = \det \left[\begin{array}{rr} a \amp b \\ 0 \amp -\frac{c}{a} b + d \\ \end{array}\right] = a (-\frac{c}{a} b + d) = ad-bc\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Note that the derivation of a formula for the determinant of a \(2 \by 2 \) matrix relied on two facts: (a) replacement doesn‚Äôt change the determinant, and (b) the determinant of a triangular matrix is the product of the diagonal elements.
    Now that we have a formula for the determinant of a \(2 \by 2 \) matrix in hand, we can develop the method of cofactor expansion. Using a cofactor expansion to find the determinant of a more general \(n\by n\) matrix is a little more work so we will demonstrate it with an example.
    Example 4.5.16.
    We illustrate how to use a cofactor expansion to find the determinant of \(A\) where
    \begin{equation*} A = \left[\begin{array}{rrr} 1 \amp -1 \amp 2 \\ -2 \amp 2 \amp -6 \\ 3 \amp -1 \amp 10 \\ \end{array}\right]. \end{equation*}
    To begin, we choose one row or column. It doesn‚Äôt matter which we choose because the result will be the same in any case. Here, we choose the second row
    \begin{equation*} \left[\begin{array}{rrr} \lgray{1} \amp \lgray{-1} \amp \lgray{2} \\ -2 \amp 2 \amp -6 \\ \lgray{3} \amp \lgray{-1} \amp \lgray{10} \\ \end{array}\right]\text{.} \end{equation*}
    The determinant will be found by creating a sum of terms, one for each entry in the row we have chosen. For each entry in the row, we form its term by multiplying
    • \((-1)^{i+j}\) where \(i\) and \(j\) are the row and column numbers, respectively, of the entry,
    • the entry itself, and
    • the determinant of the entries left over when we have crossed out the row and column containing the entry.
    Since we are computing the determinant of this matrix
    \begin{equation*} \left[\begin{array}{rrr} \gray{1} \amp \gray{-1} \amp \gray{2} \\ -2 \amp 2 \amp -6 \\ \gray{3} \amp \gray{-1} \amp \gray{10} \\ \end{array}\right] \end{equation*}
    using the second row, the entry in the first column of this row is \(-2\text{.}\) Let‚Äôs see how to form the term from this entry.
    The term itself is \(-2\text{,}\) and the matrix that is left over when we cross out the second row and first column is
    \begin{equation*} \left[\begin{array}{rrr} \gray{1} \amp {-1} \amp {2} \\ \gray{-2} \amp \gray{2} \amp \gray{-6} \\ \gray{3} \amp {-1} \amp {10} \\ \end{array}\right] \end{equation*}
    whose determinant is
    \begin{equation*} \det\left[\begin{array}{rr} -1 \amp 2 \\ -1 \amp 10 \\ \end{array}\right] = -1(10) - 2 (-1) = -8\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Since this entry is in the second row and first column, the term we construct is \((-1)^{2+1}(-2)(-8) = -16 \text{.}\)
    Putting this together, we find the determinant to be
    \begin{equation*} \begin{aligned} \left[\begin{array}{rrr} {1} \amp {-1} \amp {2} \\ -2 \amp {2} \amp {-6} \\ {3} \amp {-1} \amp {10} \\ \end{array}\right] {}={} \amp (-1)^{2+1}(-2)\det\left[\begin{array}{rr} -1 \amp 2 \\ -1 \amp 10 \\ \end{array}\right] \\ \amp {}+{} (-1)^{2+2}(2)\det\left[\begin{array}{rr} 1 \amp 2 \\ 3 \amp 10 \\ \end{array}\right] \\ \amp {}+{} (-1)^{2+3}(-6)\det\left[\begin{array}{rr} -1 \amp -1 \\ 3 \amp -1 \\ \end{array}\right] \\ \\ {}={} \amp (-1)(-2)(-1(10)-2(-1)) \\ \amp + (1)(2)(1(10)-2(3)) \\ \amp + (-1)(-6)((-1)(-1)-(-1)3) \\ \\ {}={} \amp -16 + 8 + 12 \\ {}={} \amp 4 \\ \end{aligned}\text{.} \end{equation*}
    Notice that this agrees with the determinant that we found for this matrix using row operations in the Activity¬†4.5.3.

    Subsection 4.5.3 Summary

    In this section, we associated a numerical quantity, the determinant, to a square matrix and showed how it tells us whether the matrix is invertible.
    • The determinant of a matrix has a geometric interpretation. In particular, when \(n=2\text{,}\) the determinant is the signed area of the parallelogram formed by the two columns of the matrix.
    • The determinant satisfies many properties, including the facts that
      • \(\det(AB) = \det(A) \det(B)\text{,}\) and
      • the determinant of a triangular matrix is equal to the product of its diagonal entries.
    • These properties helped us compute the determinant of a matrix using row operations. This also led to the important observation that the determinant of a matrix is nonzero if and only if the matrix is invertible.
    • Finally, we learned how to compute the determinant of a matrix using cofactor expansions, which will be a valuable tool for us in the next chapter.
    We have seen three ways to compute the determinant: by interpreting the determinant as a signed area or volume; by applying appropriate row operations; and by using a cofactor expansion. It‚Äôs worth spending a moment to think about the relative merits of these approaches.
    The geometric definition of the determinant tells us that the determinant is measuring a natural geometric quantity, an insight that does not easily come through the other two approaches. The intuition we gain by thinking about the determinant geometrically makes it seem reasonable that the determinant should be zero for matrices that are not invertible: if the columns are linearly dependent, the vectors cannot create a positive volume.
    Approaching the determinant through row operations provides an effective means of computing the determinant. In fact, this is what most computer programs do behind the scenes when they compute a determinant. This approach is also a useful theoretical tool for explaining why the determinant tells us whether a matrix is invertible.
    The cofactor expansion method will be useful to us in the next chapter when we look at eigenvalues and eigenvectors. It is not, however, a practical way to compute a determinant. To see why, consider the fact that the determinant of a \(2\by2\) matrix, written as \(ad-bc\text{,}\) requires us to compute two terms, \(ad\) and \(bc\text{.}\) To compute the determinant of a \(3\by3\) matrix, we need to compute three \(2\by2\) determinants, which involves \(3\cdot 2 = 6\) terms. For a \(4\by4\) matrix, we need to compute four \(3\by3\) determinants, which produces \(4\cdot3\cdot2 = 24\) terms. Continuing in this way, we see that the cofactor expansion of a \(10\by10\) matrix would involve \(10\cdot9\cdot8\ldots3\cdot2 = 10! = 3628800\) terms.
    By contrast, we have seen that the number of steps required to perform Gaussian elimination on an \(n\by n\) matrix is proportional to \(n^3\text{.}\) When \(n=10\text{,}\) we have \(n^3 = 1000\text{,}\) which points to the fact that finding the determinant using Gaussian elimination is considerably less work.
