The vector of ones and the \(\xvec_i\) vectors form the columns of the \(n \by (p+1)\) matrix \(X\text{.}\) This matrix is usually refered to as the model matrix. The coefficients \(\beta_i\) are arranged into a column vector. Then the entire relationship is expressed as a simple equation of the form \(A\xvec = \bvec\text{,}\) but with different letters. And typically statisticians prefer to swap the left and right sides of the equation as well. So the model is exprssed like this:
\begin{align*} X \amp = \begin{bmatrix} \onevec \; \xvec_1 \; \xvec_2 \; \cdots \; \xvec_p \end{bmatrix} \; , \quad \betavec = \begin{bmatrix} \beta_0 \\ \beta_1 \\ \beta_2 \\ \vdots \\ \beta_p \end{bmatrix}\\ \yvec \amp = X \betavec\text{.} \end{align*}
This equation will almost never have an exact solution because in typical applications \(X\) will have many more rows than columns.
