We will motivate the defintion of the dot product by investigating the projection of a vector \(\yvec\) in the direction of a vector \(\xvec\text{,}\) which we will denote as \(\proj{\yvec}{\xvec}\text{.}\) The projection vector \(\proj{\yvec}{\xvec}\) is the scalar multiple of \(\xvec\text{,}\)
\begin{equation*} \proj{\yvec}{\xvec} = k \xvec \end{equation*}
for the scalar \(k\) such that \(k \xvec\) and \((\yvec - k \xvec)\) form a 90 degree angle. (See [cross-reference to target(s) "fig-def-projection" missing or not unique].) By the definition of cosine, this means that
\begin{equation*} \frac{k \len{\xvec}}{\len{\yvec}} = \cos \theta \end{equation*}
where \(\len{\xvec}\) denotes the (Euclidean) length of the vector \(\xvec\) and \(\theta\) is the angle between the two vectors. Solving for \(k\text{,}\) we find that
\begin{equation*} k = \frac{\len{\yvec} \cos \theta}{\len{\xvec}}\text{.} \end{equation*}
So
\begin{equation*} \proj{\yvec}{\xvec} = \frac{\len{\yvec} \cos \theta}{\len{\xvec}} \xvec\text{.} \end{equation*}
