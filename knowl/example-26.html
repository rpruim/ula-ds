Example 4.1.6.

Consider the following two matrices
\begin{equation*} A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp 2\\ 2 \amp 2 \amp 1\\ \end{bmatrix} \quad B = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0\\ -1 \amp 0.5 \\ 0 \amp 0\\ \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Then
\begin{align*} AB \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0\\ 0 \amp 1\\ \end{bmatrix} = I_2\\ BA \amp = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \amp 0 \amp 2\\ 0 \amp 1 \amp -1.5\\ 0 \amp 0 \amp 0\\ \end{bmatrix} \neq I_3 \end{align*}
So \(B\) is a right inverse of \(A\text{,}\) but \(B\) is not a left inverse of \(A\text{.}\)
Now consider
\begin{equation*} B_2 = \begin{bmatrix} -3 \amp -4 \\ 2 \amp 3.5 \\ 2 \amp 2 \\ \end{bmatrix}\text{.} \end{equation*}
Multiplication shows that
\begin{equation*} A B_2 = I_2\text{,} \end{equation*}
So \(A\) has more than one right inverse. In fact, there are infinitely many and none of them can be a left inverse, since if \(L\) had both a left and a right inverse, they would have to be the same by PropositionÂ 4.1.2. But that means a left inverse would have to be the same as both \(B\) and \(B_2\text{,}\) which is not posssible.
